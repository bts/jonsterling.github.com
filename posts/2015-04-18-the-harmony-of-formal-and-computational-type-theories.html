The harmony of formal and computational type theories.

Since the 1980s, Martin-Löf has differentiated between two separate concepts of “type”; the first is a classifier of expressions by syntactic category, and the second is a semantic tool which is adequate for building up intuitionistic mathematics. Both are formulated using the judgemental method, but differ strongly in how they interpret hypothetical judgement; it will be informative to expound both theories, and show how they form in their harmony a two-level (syntactic-semantic) foundation for intuitionistic mathematics.

Hypothetical and general judgement

There are two basic kinds of higher-order judgement which may be dealt with. The first is hypothetical judgement \({\mathcal{J}_2\ (\mathcal{J}_1)}\). The meaning explanation for hypothetical judgement is as follows:

To know \({\mathcal{J}_2\ (\mathcal{J}_1)}\) is to know \(\mathcal{J}_2\) under the circumstances that you know \(\mathcal{J}_1\).

An inference rule \({\dfrac{\mathcal{J}_1}{\mathcal{J}_2}}\) should be taken to represent an evident hypothetical judgement.

It’s crucial to note that the meaning of this judgement corresponds to admissibility, not derivability; that is to say, in coming to know a hypothetical judgement, you may perform “case analysis” on the evidence for the hypothesis. So the evidence for a hypothetical judgement may be thought of as a computational (meta-)function which maps derivations of the hypothesis to derivations of the conclusion.

The other kind of higher-order judgement is general judgement \({\lvert_{{\color{MidnightBlue}{x}}}\,\mathcal{J}(x)}\); its meaning explanation is as follows:

To know \({\lvert_{{\color{MidnightBlue}{x}}}\,\mathcal{J}(x)}\) is to know \(\mathcal{J}(c)\) for each substitution \(c\) for \(x\).

The evidence for such a general judgement is an object which is evidence for \(\mathcal{J}(x)\) for any arbitrary substitution of \(x\).

In practice, general judgements are often supressed and replaced with schematic rules. For instance, we may signify the evident assertion \({\lvert_{{\color{MidnightBlue}{m}}}\,{\mathcal{J}_2(m)\ (\mathcal{J}_1(m))}}\) with the schematic rule \[ {\dfrac{ \mathcal{J}_1(m) }{ \mathcal{J}_2(m) }} \]

The syntactic types

To define a syntactic type, you must propound what it means for something to be an object of that type and when two such objects are equal. That \(c\) is an object of a type \(\alpha\) is expressed by the assertion \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{c}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\alpha}}}\), and that \(c,d\) are equal objects of a type \(\alpha\) is expressed by the assertion \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{c}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{d}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\alpha}}}\).

In practice, a syntactic type is defined inductively, and the meanings of the assertions \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{c}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\alpha}}}, {{\color{MidnightBlue}{c}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{d}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\alpha}}}\) are purely formally (that is to say, they are defined entirely and solely by the categorical rules which have them as their conclusion). For instance, the type of numerals \({\mathbf{num}}\) is often defined as follows:

\[\begin{gather} {\dfrac{ }{ {{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathsf{0}}}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}} }}\qquad {\dfrac{ {{\color{MidnightBlue}{n}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}} }{ {{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathsf{s}(n)}}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}} }}\\[6pt] {\dfrac{ }{ {{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathsf{0}}}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathsf{0}}}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}} }}\qquad {\dfrac{ {{\color{MidnightBlue}{n}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{m}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}} }{ {{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathsf{s}(n)}}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathsf{s}(m)}}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}} }} \end{gather}\]

However, there is a problem with the above definition if we try to make the actual asserted judgements fully explicit in the case of the successor; the second rule must be taken to represent the following evident judgement, based on our earlier definition of an “inference rule” as an evident hypothetical judgement:

\[ {\lvert_{{\color{MidnightBlue}{n}}}\,{{{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathsf{s}(n)}}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}}\ ({{\color{MidnightBlue}{n}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}})}} \]

Indeed, the judgement is evident based on the intended denotation of the type \({\mathbf{num}}\), but it is not a definition. It is merely an inference from a definition, we we seem to have tacitly assumed. To make it explicit, we will give a proper definition of the type \({\mathbf{num}}\):

A canonical object of \({\mathbf{num}}\) is either \({\mathsf{0}}\) or \({\mathsf{s}(n)}\) such that \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{n}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}}\); \({\mathsf{0}}\) is equal to \({\mathsf{0}}\), and \({\mathsf{s}(n)}\) is equal to \({\mathsf{s}(m)}\) when \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{n}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{m}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}}\).

A failed attempt at a representational function type

In order to introduce terms that have binding forms, we will need a “function type”. At first, we may attempt to define it using hypothetical and general judgement as follows:

* A canonical object of \((\alpha)\beta\) (presupposing \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{\alpha}}:\mathit{type}}\), \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{\beta}}:\mathit{type}}\)) is a term \([x]e(x)\) such that \({\lvert_{{\color{MidnightBlue}{x}}}\,{{{\color{MidnightBlue}{e(x)}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\beta}}}\ ({{\color{MidnightBlue}{x}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\alpha}}})}}\) and \({\lvert_{{\color{MidnightBlue}{x,y}}}\,{{{\color{MidnightBlue}{e(x)}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{e(y)}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\beta}}}\ ({{\color{MidnightBlue}{x}}={\color{MidnightBlue}{y}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{\alpha}}})}}\).

This is indeed an adequate function type, however it does not suffice to represent binding. For instance, if we wished to represent a numeric expression with a free variable \(x\), we might use the type \(({\mathbf{num}}){\mathbf{num}}\) that we have described above, and indeed, it allows us to know the judgement \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{[x]{\mathsf{s}({\mathsf{s}(x)})}}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{({\mathbf{num}}){\mathbf{num}}}}}\), but nothing stops us from defining a constant \(\mathsf{bad}\) at the meta-level, and then coming to know the judgement \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{[x]\mathsf{bad}(x)}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{({\mathbf{num}}){\mathbf{num}}}}}\) through inductive reasoning:

I know \({{\color{MidnightBlue}{[x]\mathsf{bad}(x)}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{({\mathbf{num}}){\mathbf{num}}}}}\), since it suffices to know \({\lvert_{{\color{MidnightBlue}{x}}}\,{{{\color{MidnightBlue}{\mathsf{bad}(x)}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}}\ ({{\color{MidnightBlue}{x}}:{\color{MidnightBlue}{{\mathbf{num}}}}})}}\); I know this, because I have defined \(\mathsf{bad}({\mathsf{0}}) \equiv {\mathsf{0}}\) and \(\mathsf{bad}({\mathsf{s}(n)}) \equiv n\), whence I know the above hypothetical judgement. (extensionality premise omitted for space)

This is clearly not what we want. In order to get a proper treatment of binding and substitution, then, it will be necessary to define a formal theory of expressions with binding (a logical framework).

The logical framework

The rules of the logical framework presented here are to be taken purely formally: that is to say, the meanings of its judgements are entirely defined by the rules which have them as their conclusion.
